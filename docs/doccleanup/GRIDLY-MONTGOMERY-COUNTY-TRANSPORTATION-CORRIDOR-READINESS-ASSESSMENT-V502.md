# GRIDLY Montgomery County Transportation & Corridor Readiness Assessment V502

## 1. Executive Summary

V502 is a documentation-only transportation and corridor readiness assessment for Montgomery County, Texas as the County #2 candidate. Its purpose is to assess whether Montgomery County can satisfy the transportation, corridor, mobility, routing, crossing, and county-containment assumptions required for a future County #2 onboarding effort.

V502 builds on the V459 through V475 County Platform Foundation, V500 Montgomery County Candidate Assessment, and V501 Montgomery County Geographic & Boundary Readiness Assessment. V500 established Montgomery County as a viable County #2 candidate at a broad planning level. V501 narrowed that review to geographic, boundary, ownership, and containment readiness. V502 now evaluates whether Montgomery County's transportation structure appears compatible with the same non-activation, evidence-first, county-contained expansion model.

The transportation-readiness objective is to determine whether Montgomery County's highway, state route, FM road, rail, commuter, growth-corridor, Route Watch, and cross-county movement patterns appear suitable for later governed assessment before any package, registry, storage, fixture, migration, runtime, Supabase, historical, DriveTexas, Transportation Intelligence, Route Watch, corridor activation, or ingestion work is considered.

V502 does not:

- Activate Montgomery County.
- Create a Montgomery County package.
- Create registry entries.
- Create storage namespaces.
- Create fixtures.
- Create migrations.
- Modify runtime behavior.
- Modify Supabase.
- Enable historical reads.
- Enable history UI.
- Enable historical APIs.
- Resume DriveTexas.
- Enable Transportation Intelligence.
- Authorize transportation data ingestion.
- Authorize Route Watch expansion.
- Authorize corridor activation.
- Create crossing inventories.
- Authorize user-visible county selection.
- Authorize county-scoped reads or writes.

All findings in this milestone are assessment observations only.

## 2. Transportation Network Assessment

V502 evaluates Montgomery County's transportation network at a planning level only. It does not ingest roadway data, validate geometry, create source contracts, normalize corridors, create route records, or enable transportation features.

### 2.1 Major highways

Montgomery County appears to contain several major highway corridors that are strategically relevant to future County #2 readiness:

| Corridor | Planning-level relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Interstate 45 | Primary north-south regional commuter corridor connecting Houston-area movement with The Woodlands, Conroe, Willis, and points north. |
| Interstate 69 / U.S. Highway 59 | Eastern Montgomery and Liberty-adjacent regional corridor supporting New Caney, Porter, Splendora, Cleveland-area, and broader northeast Houston movement. |
| State Highway 99 / Grand Parkway | Regional loop corridor with high growth, cross-county movement, and future route-awareness relevance. |

These corridors create strong future assessment value because they combine commuter traffic, incident sensitivity, regional routing, edge-county movement, and growth pressure. They also increase containment risk because a single traveler route may interact with Montgomery, Liberty, Harris, and other counties.

Assessment finding: **Major highway readiness appears favorable, with high containment and governance requirements before any operational use.**

### 2.2 Major state highways

Major state highway corridors likely relevant to future Montgomery assessment include SH 105, SH 242, SH 249, SH 75, and SH 99. These corridors support east-west movement, local access, Houston-region commuting, county-seat access, growth areas, and Liberty-adjacent travel patterns.

SH 105 is especially relevant for Conroe-to-Cleveland and Montgomery-to-Liberty movement. SH 242 is relevant for I-45 to eastern Montgomery and U.S. 59 / I-69 connectivity. SH 249 is relevant for western and southwestern Montgomery growth and commuter movement. SH 75 is relevant as a legacy north-south local corridor parallel to I-45.

Assessment finding: **State highway structure appears suitable for future evidence-based corridor planning, but none of these corridors are activated or ingested by V502.**

### 2.3 Major FM roads

Montgomery County's FM road network is likely important for future county-owned awareness because many communities, subdivisions, rural areas, schools, retail nodes, and commuter connections depend on farm-to-market roads rather than only interstate or state highway corridors.

Candidate FM corridors for future evidence review may include FM 1488, FM 2978, FM 1314, FM 3083, FM 1097, FM 830, FM 2854, FM 1485, FM 2090, and other locally important connectors. V502 does not validate this list as package evidence and does not create any corridor inventory.

Assessment finding: **FM-road complexity appears moderate to high and should be handled through future source-backed package evidence only.**

### 2.4 Major regional corridors

Montgomery County participates in several regional corridor patterns:

- North-south Houston, The Woodlands, Conroe, Willis, and Walker County movement.
- Northeast Houston, New Caney, Porter, Splendora, Cleveland, Liberty County, and I-69 / U.S. 59 movement.
- East-west Conroe, Cleveland, Lake Conroe, and county-edge movement.
- Grand Parkway movement involving Montgomery, Harris, Liberty-area, and broader Houston-region travel.
- Western and southwestern growth movement involving Magnolia, SH 249, FM 1488, and related corridors.

These regional patterns make Montgomery useful for testing whether the County Platform Foundation can reason about county-owned readiness without treating regional movement as an excuse to blend county ownership.

Assessment finding: **Regional corridor relevance is high and strategically useful for future County #2 validation.**

### 2.5 Major commuter corridors

Major commuter corridors likely include I-45, I-69 / U.S. 59, SH 99, SH 249, SH 242, SH 105, FM 1488, FM 2978, FM 1314, and related local connectors. Commuter behavior is likely strongest between Montgomery County, Harris County, The Woodlands, Conroe, Houston-area employment centers, and eastern Montgomery / Liberty-adjacent communities.

Assessment finding: **Commuter-corridor complexity is high enough to require explicit future containment and Route Watch governance.**

## 3. Rail Corridor Assessment

### 3.1 Major rail presence

Montgomery County includes rail corridor considerations that may be relevant to future awareness, routing, freight disruption, and crossing planning. V502 does not identify authoritative rail geometry, create rail source contracts, create crossing records, or validate rail assets.

Assessment finding: **Rail presence appears relevant for future assessment, pending authoritative source review.**

### 3.2 Corridor relevance

Rail corridors may matter because they can interact with roadway mobility, emergency routing, industrial or freight movement, and localized traffic interruptions. Their relevance is not limited to rail users; roadway users may be affected by rail-adjacent congestion, blocked crossings, construction, maintenance, or incidents.

Assessment finding: **Rail-corridor relevance is moderate and should remain future-only until governed evidence exists.**

### 3.3 Crossing relevance

Rail crossings may be important for future county-owned awareness because grade crossings can create localized delay, emergency access constraints, and alternate-route needs. However, V502 explicitly does not create crossing inventories, crossing fixtures, crossing IDs, crossing maps, or crossing-related runtime behavior.

Assessment finding: **Crossing relevance is likely meaningful, but crossing inventory work is not authorized.**

### 3.4 Awareness implications

Future awareness planning may need to account for rail-related disruptions if Montgomery County proceeds toward package design. Any such work would require governed source selection, county containment, corridor ownership rules, and clear user-facing boundaries before activation.

Assessment finding: **Rail awareness implications are suitable for later assessment, not implementation.**

## 4. Corridor Complexity Assessment

### 4.1 Urban corridors

Urban and suburban corridor complexity is concentrated around The Woodlands, Shenandoah, Oak Ridge North, Conroe, New Caney, Porter, and other high-growth areas. These corridors may involve dense traffic, overlapping municipal identities, school and retail trip patterns, medical and employment destinations, and frequent alternate-route decisions.

Assessment finding: **Urban corridor complexity is high but useful for validating awareness and containment assumptions.**

### 4.2 Rural corridors

Rural and exurban corridors across northern, western, northeastern, and lake-adjacent portions of Montgomery County may have longer distances, fewer alternate routes, lower report density, and stronger dependence on state highways and FM roads. These characteristics can increase the importance of county-wide and corridor-level awareness.

Assessment finding: **Rural corridor complexity is moderate and requires future evidence-based planning.**

### 4.3 Growth corridors

Growth corridors are a central transportation-readiness consideration for Montgomery County. SH 99, SH 249, I-45, SH 242, I-69 / U.S. 59, SH 105, FM 1488, FM 2978, and FM 1314 may all involve changing travel demand, new development, construction sensitivity, and evolving commuter patterns.

Assessment finding: **Growth-corridor complexity is high and should be revisited before any future activation decision.**

### 4.4 Freight corridors

Freight relevance may occur along interstate, U.S. highway, state highway, industrial, rail-adjacent, and regional movement corridors. Freight behavior can affect incident severity, closure impact, alternate-route suitability, and county-boundary routing.

Assessment finding: **Freight-corridor relevance is moderate and should be assessed only through future governed source evidence.**

### 4.5 Commuter corridors

Commuter corridors are likely the highest-value transportation-readiness category because Montgomery County has strong relationships with The Woodlands, Conroe, Houston-area employment centers, eastern Montgomery communities, Liberty-adjacent travel, and broader regional movement.

Assessment finding: **Commuter corridor complexity is high and supports continued readiness assessment.**

## 5. Route Watch Assessment

V502 evaluates Route Watch relevance only. It does not modify Route Watch, expand Route Watch, create route geometry, activate destination ownership, change scoring, enable route intelligence, or authorize runtime behavior.

### 5.1 Potential Route Watch relevance

Montgomery County appears highly relevant to future Route Watch planning because residents and travelers may depend on repeat routes along I-45, I-69 / U.S. 59, SH 99, SH 105, SH 242, SH 249, and FM connectors. Route Watch could eventually need to reason about county-owned route relevance, destination ownership, commuter direction, and corridor disruptions.

Assessment finding: **Potential Route Watch relevance is high, but remains non-operational.**

### 5.2 Cross-county commuting implications

Many plausible Montgomery routes may cross into Harris, Liberty, Walker, San Jacinto, Waller, or Grimes counties. That makes a simple county-only route assumption insufficient for future design. A future Route Watch expansion would need clear rules for whether a route is Montgomery-owned, cross-county, Liberty-owned, Harris-adjacent, or regional-only.

Assessment finding: **Cross-county commuting creates high governance requirements before Route Watch expansion.**

### 5.3 County-containment implications

Route Watch must not become a back door for county data leakage. Future implementation would need to prove that Montgomery route relevance does not expose Liberty-owned data, Liberty route relevance does not expose Montgomery-owned data, and regional commuter context does not bypass county containment.

Assessment finding: **County-containment implications are high and must be validated before any Route Watch changes.**

### 5.4 Liberty-to-Montgomery route interactions

Liberty-to-Montgomery route interactions may involve SH 105, I-69 / U.S. 59, SH 99, New Caney, Porter, Splendora, Cleveland-area movement, and local connectors near the shared county edge. These interactions are useful for future validation because they can test whether adjacent counties remain distinct while still acknowledging real travel behavior.

Assessment finding: **Liberty-to-Montgomery Route Watch relevance is high, but no Route Watch modification is authorized.**

## 6. Liberty-to-Montgomery Transportation Interaction Assessment

### 6.1 Shared travel patterns

Shared travel patterns are likely to include work trips, school trips, service access, shopping, healthcare, emergency response, regional highway travel, and recreational movement. These patterns may cross the Montgomery-Liberty boundary or pass through Liberty-adjacent Montgomery communities.

Assessment finding: **Shared travel patterns are expected and should be modeled deliberately in future phases.**

### 6.2 Shared corridors

Potential shared or interacting corridors include SH 105, I-69 / U.S. 59, SH 99, FM 2090, FM 1485, and local connections involving Splendora, New Caney, Porter, Cleveland-area travel, and the Montgomery-Liberty boundary. V502 does not validate corridor geometry or ownership.

Assessment finding: **Shared corridor interaction is likely meaningful and requires future source-backed validation.**

### 6.3 Commuter movement

Commuter movement between Liberty and Montgomery may be less dominant than Montgomery-to-Harris movement, but it is strategically important because County #1 and County #2 adjacency must not create ownership ambiguity. Even low-volume or edge-case routes can test containment rules.

Assessment finding: **Liberty-to-Montgomery commuter movement is a moderate operational risk and a high validation opportunity.**

### 6.4 County-boundary edge cases

Potential transportation edge cases include:

- A route beginning in Liberty County and ending in Montgomery County.
- A route beginning in Montgomery County and ending in Liberty County.
- A traveler passing through Montgomery while the trip purpose is Harris- or Liberty-oriented.
- A disruption on a corridor near the county line that is relevant to both counties.
- A corridor whose user-facing name is regional but whose future package ownership must remain county-scoped.
- A rail or roadway crossing close enough to a county edge to create ambiguous awareness relevance.

Assessment finding: **Boundary edge cases are foreseeable and compatible with future readiness validation.**

## 8. Transportation Readiness Risks

| Risk area | Risk description | Assessment severity | Mitigation direction |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Corridor risks | Major corridors may be regional rather than purely county-local, increasing ownership ambiguity. | High | Define county-owned, cross-county, and regional-context corridor rules before activation. |
| Rail risks | Rail crossings and freight movement may affect roadway awareness, emergency access, and delay patterns. | Moderate | Evaluate authoritative rail and crossing sources in a future governed milestone only. |
| Commuter risks | Commuter routes may routinely cross Montgomery, Liberty, Harris, and other county boundaries. | High | Require explicit Route Watch containment and cross-county route governance. |
| Containment risks | Transportation features could leak adjacent-county relevance into Montgomery-owned contexts. | High | Validate V463-style read, write, route, and awareness containment before runtime use. |
| Growth-corridor risks | Rapidly changing corridors may make static assumptions stale. | Moderate | Version future package evidence and require periodic source review. |
| Future scalability risks | Montgomery's corridor density may expose limits in county package, awareness, and route models. | Moderate | Use Montgomery as a structured County #2 stress test before broader expansion. |
| FM-road risks | Local and FM-road importance may be underestimated if future work focuses only on major highways. | Moderate | Include FM roads in future evidence planning without activating them prematurely. |
| Crossing risks | Future crossing awareness could become operationally sensitive without validated inventories. | Moderate | Do not create crossing inventories until source, governance, and containment rules are approved. |

## 9. Transportation Readiness Determination

Determination: **HIGH READINESS**

Rationale:

- Montgomery County has major transportation corridors that are strategically useful for County #2 readiness assessment.
- The county includes interstate, U.S. highway, state highway, FM-road, commuter, growth, and rail-adjacent complexity suitable for testing the County Platform Foundation.
- Liberty-adjacent movement creates a valuable containment test without requiring immediate activation.
- Corridor risks are significant but appear governable through future source-backed evidence, fixture design, Route Watch governance, and V463-style containment validation.
- No transportation concern identified in V502 appears to disqualify Montgomery County from continuing the readiness sequence.

This high-readiness determination is not activation authority. It means Montgomery County remains transportation-suitable for the next documentation-only readiness phase.

## 10. Recommendation

Recommendation: **Ready with observations**

Montgomery County should proceed to the next documentation-only readiness assessment phase, with the following observations:

- Future work must treat major highways, state highways, FM roads, rail corridors, and crossings as evidence-backed package candidates only after governance approval.
- Route Watch implications should remain explicitly disabled until county containment and cross-county route ownership are proven.
- Liberty-to-Montgomery route interactions should be treated as a primary validation scenario.
- Harris County and broader Houston-region commuter context should remain regional context unless separately governed.
- Growth corridors should be reviewed again before any future package or activation decision.

## 12. Final Determination

Based on transportation and corridor considerations, Montgomery County continues to appear suitable as County #2.

The county appears to have a transportation network that is complex enough to validate future county containment, awareness, Route Watch, rail, crossing, commuter, and growth-corridor assumptions, while still remaining suitable for a governed documentation-first readiness sequence. The risks identified in V502 are real, especially around cross-county commuting, regional corridors, rail/crossing awareness, Route Watch, and containment, but they appear manageable through future evidence collection, fixture design, governance review, dry-run execution, and decision simulation.

V502 grants no activation authority and authorizes no implementation.
